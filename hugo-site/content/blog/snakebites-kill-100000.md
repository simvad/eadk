Snakebites kill 100,000 people every year. When I first heard this I refused to believe it. How had I managed to call myself an EA for so many years and not known snakebites were this significant a cause of death?

Snakebite envenoming is one of the most neglected tropical diseases. It primarily affects the world's poorest and most vulnerable populations - agricultural workers in rural areas of developing countries. The World Health Organization has classified it as a "neglected tropical disease of the highest priority."

## The Scale of the Problem

- **5.4 million** people are bitten by snakes each year
- **1.8-2.7 million** develop clinical illness from envenoming
- **81,000-138,000** die from snakebite annually
- **400,000** survivors suffer permanent disabilities

## Why Is This So Neglected?

Snakebite disproportionately affects poor, rural communities who have little political voice. The victims are often subsistence farmers and their children. Because these populations have limited purchasing power, pharmaceutical companies have little financial incentive to develop and produce antivenoms.

## What Can Be Done?

Research into better, more affordable antivenoms is ongoing. Organizations working on this issue include the WHO's snakebite envenoming initiative and various research institutions working on next-generation antivenoms.

This is a powerful example of how a massive global health problem can fly under the radar - and why the EA framework of looking at scale, neglectedness, and tractability is so valuable for identifying where we can do the most good.
